Visual Data Story: Observational Fashion Survey

Plot 1: Dominant Outfit Colours by Observed Locations

This plot shows the distribution of dominant outfit colours including black, white, blue, neutral tones, and bright colours observed across different locations. Black was the most common colour of clothing on the University Campus. This could be because students like it because it’s useful and can be worn with many different outfits.Neutral tones were frequently noticed in places such as cafes or outdoor settings, indicating a shift towards calmer or peaceful aesthetics in those environments.

Plot 2: Average Number of Clothing Layers by Hour of Day

The amount of layers of clothing that are visible varies during the day, as shown in this line graph. There is a noticeable reduction in layering during midday, probably due to indoor environments during lunch breaks or rising temperatures. In the early morning and late afternoon, layering increases, possibly due to commuting or cooler weather. This time cycle demonstrates how social and environmental rhythms, such as those at work or lectures, impact attire choices.

Plot 3: Number of Visible Logos by Gender Presentation

This box plot illustrates how many logos are shown in presentations for each gender. Those seen with a masculine presentation generally had a somewhat higher number of visible logos. In contrast, individuals with a feminine presentation showed a wider range of logo counts, including several with none. The box plot layout allows us to see variability and outliers, demonstrating averages and how brand visibility choices may change by gender.

Plot 4: Average Layers by Hour at the University Campus

This line graph focuses on the University Campus and shows how layering varies over time in this specific location. Earlier in the day, the average number of layers tend to be higher but then this decrease slightly toward midday, then rise again later. The trend is similar to the pattern in Plot 2, but more pronounced, possibly from the longer outdoor walks between classes and the change in lecture hall temperatures.
